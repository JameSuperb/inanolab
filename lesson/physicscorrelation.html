Distance vs Time

Explore how motion shapes the distance-time graph — linear or not

Physics Principle

A distance-time graph shows how an object's position changes over time. Its shape is not always linear — it depends entirely on how the object is moving.

• Constant velocity → straight line (linear). Equal distances covered in equal time intervals.

• Negative constant velocity → straight line with a negative slope. Position decreases by equal amounts in equal time intervals (motion in the opposite direction).

• Speeding up (acceleration) → upward-curving line. Distances grow larger with each time step.

• Slowing down (deceleration) → flattening curve. Distances grow smaller with each time step.

• Stationary → horizontal line. No change in distance over time.

The slope (gradient) at any point on the graph equals the object's instantaneous velocity at that moment. Positive slope means positive velocity, negative slope means negative velocity, and a steeper slope means higher speed magnitude.

Variables

Independent: Time (s) — the quantity you control or measure at regular intervals.

Dependent: Distance (m) — how far the object has traveled from its starting point.

Observed: Velocity (m/s) — inferred from the slope of the graph. It may be constant, increasing, or decreasing depending on the motion.

Possible Relationships

The graph shape changes with the type of motion:

Constant velocity: d = v × t  (linear)
Constant acceleration: d = ½ a × t²  (quadratic)

In general, the slope at any point = instantaneous velocity. Average velocity over an interval = Δd / Δt.

Real-World Applications

• GPS tracking of vehicles — speed inferred from changing position over time

• Sports science — analyzing sprinter acceleration versus steady-pace phases

• Traffic engineering — detecting congestion from non-linear distance data

• Physics experiments — distinguishing uniform from non-uniform motion using sensor data

Velocity vs Time

Explore how changing motion shapes the velocity-time graph — linear or not

Physics Principle

A velocity-time graph shows how an object's speed and direction change over time. Its shape is not always linear — it depends entirely on how the acceleration varies.

• Zero acceleration (constant velocity) → horizontal line. Speed does not change.

• Constant acceleration → straight diagonal line. Velocity changes by equal amounts each second.

• Increasing acceleration → upward-curving line. Velocity grows faster and faster.

• Decreasing acceleration → flattening curve. Velocity still grows, but more and more slowly.

• Zero velocity (stationary) → point on or along the time axis (v = 0).

The slope (gradient) at any point equals the object's instantaneous acceleration at that moment. A steeper slope means greater acceleration.

Variables

Independent: Time (s) — the quantity measured at regular intervals.

Dependent: Velocity (m/s) — how fast the object is moving at each moment in time.

Observed: Acceleration (m/s²) — inferred from the slope of the graph. It may be constant, increasing, decreasing, or zero depending on the forces acting on the object.

Possible Relationships

The graph shape changes with the type of motion:

Constant acceleration: v = v₀ + a × t  (linear)
Non-constant acceleration: v = f(t)  (curve)

The area under a velocity-time graph gives the displacement of the object over that time interval.

Real-World Applications

• Car speedometer logs — speed varies freely during a trip, rarely linear

• Rocket launches — thrust decreases as fuel burns, so acceleration (and the slope) changes over time

• Athlete sprint analysis — explosive start, peak speed, then deceleration near the finish

• Roller coaster dynamics — velocity rises and falls in curves throughout the ride

Ohm's Law

Exploring the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance

Physics Principle

Ohm's Law describes the fundamental relationship in electrical circuits between voltage, current, and resistance.

For a given resistance, voltage is directly proportional to current.

Variables

Independent: Voltage (V)

Dependent: Current (A)

Control: Resistance (Ω)

Expected Relationship

Linear relationship between voltage and current:

V = I × R

Rearranged: I = V / R (slope = 1/R)

Real-World Applications

• Electrical circuit design

• Device power consumption

• Wire sizing for safety

• LED brightness control

Frequency & Wavelength

Discovering the inverse relationship in waves

Physics Principle

For waves traveling at constant velocity, frequency and wavelength are inversely proportional.

Higher frequency waves have shorter wavelengths, and vice versa.

Variables

Independent: Frequency (Hz)

Dependent: Wavelength (m)

Control: Wave velocity (m/s)

Expected Relationship

Inverse relationship between frequency and wavelength:

v = f × λ

Therefore: λ = v / f

Real-World Applications

• Radio signal tuning

• Sound wave analysis

• Light spectrum properties

• Acoustic design

Light Intensity

Understanding inverse square law through experimentation

Physics Principle

Light intensity follows the inverse square law: intensity decreases with the square of the distance from the source.

This fundamental principle applies to all radiation spreading spherically.

Variables

Independent: Distance (m)

Dependent: Intensity (lux or W/m²)

Control: Light source power

Expected Relationship

Inverse square relationship:

I ∝ 1/r²

Intensity = Constant / (distance)²

Real-World Applications

• Photography exposure settings

• Lighting design in architecture

• Radiation safety regulations

• Astronomical brightness calculations
