Essential Question
How can we experimentally determine the natural abundance of K-40 by measuring the radioactivity of a KCl sample?

Why this matters

  • Natural potassium contains stable isotopes and the radioactive isotope K-40.
  • KCl is a convenient potassium-containing sample, so its gamma emission can reveal the abundance of K-40.
  • The experiment connects activity → number of K-40 nuclei → total K nuclei → natural abundance.

Roadmap

  1. Determine the effective detector distance.
  2. Build the detector efficiency calibration curve.
  3. Measure the 1460 keV gamma peak from KCl.
  4. Calculate the total potassium nuclei in 50 mg of KCl.
  5. Compute the natural abundance of K-40.
Scientific Background

A = λN

A is activity, λ is decay constant, and N is the number of radioactive nuclei.

Why the 1460 keV peak matters

K-40 emits a characteristic gamma ray at 1460 keV. Measuring this peak allows us to estimate the activity of K-40 in the KCl sample.

Natural abundance of K-40 = (K-40 nuclei / total K nuclei) × 100%

The detector does not catch every emitted gamma ray, so we must calibrate detector efficiency before measuring KCl.

Geometric factor
G = Adet / (4π (x + d)2)
Here, x is the source-to-detector distance (10 cm in this step), d is the fitted effective distance, and Adet is the detector face area.
Efficiency formula
ε = R / (A · Y · G)
Where R = Counts / Time (cps), A = Activity (Bq) = Activity(µCi) × 3.7 × 104, Y = gamma yield, and G is the geometry factor computed using the fitted d from Step 4.
